# Camera & Lens Language

Precise vocabulary for describing camera choices, perspectives, framing, and compositional structures.

---

## Focal Lengths (Visual Character)

### 24mm Wide Angle
Environmental storytelling, exaggerated depth, immersive spatial drama. Pulls the viewer into the scene. Foreground objects feel close and imposing, background stretches away with theatrical perspective.

### 35mm Reportage
Natural human perspective with gentle context. Documentary authenticity. Feels honest, unmanipulated. Subject remains relatable whilst environment provides meaningful backdrop without dominating.

### 50mm Neutral Eye
Pure observation, zero distortion. What the human eye naturally sees. Balanced intimacy and context. Neither pushes nor pulls, simply witnesses. Timeless, classical framing.

### 85mm Portrait
Compressed perspective, shallow depth, isolated subject. Background melts into creamy bokeh. Intimacy without intrusion. Flattering compression of facial features. Draws attention through selective focus.

### 135mm+ Telephoto
Extreme compression, layered depth, voyeuristic distance. Foreground and background collapse together. Creates graphic, abstract quality. Removes viewer from scene—observing rather than participating.

---

## Camera Angles (Emotional Position)

### Eye-Level
Neutral relationship, equality between viewer and subject. Neither dominant nor submissive. Honest, direct, conversational.

### Low Angle
Looking up—subject gains power, monumentality, dominance. Sky expands behind subject. Heroic, imposing, authoritative. Viewer in position of deference.

### High Angle
Looking down—subject becomes vulnerable, diminished, exposed. Floor/ground expands around subject. Creates distance, surveillance quality, god's-eye objectivity.

### Bird's Eye
Directly overhead—abstract patterns emerge, human scale disappears. Geographic rather than empathetic. Maps relationships spatially rather than emotionally.

### Dutch Angle / Canted Frame
Diagonal horizon—psychological unease, instability, tension. World knocked off-balance. Noir disorientation. Use sparingly for specific emotional disruption.

---

## Framing (Compositional Intimacy)

### Extreme Wide Shot
Geography dominates, humans become environmental elements. Scale and space define mood. Epic, isolating, or overwhelming depending on context.

### Wide Shot
Full body visible with meaningful environment. Subject and space in conversation. Cinematic establishing grammar.

### Medium Shot
Waist-up framing. Conversational distance. Gestures visible, expressions readable. Workhorse framing for narrative momentum.

### Close-Up
Face fills frame. Emotional transparency. Every micro-expression visible. Intimacy or confrontation depending on context.

### Extreme Close-Up
Detail becomes abstract—eye, hand, object fragment. Texture and surface dominate. Psychological intensity or sensory focus.

---

## Compositional Patterns

### Rule of Thirds
Subject on intersecting lines, never dead centre. Creates visual tension and natural balance. Dynamic rather than static.

### Leading Lines
Architectural or natural elements guide eye through frame. Roads, hallways, shadows, light beams. Creates depth and visual journey.

### Negative Space
Intentional emptiness gives breathing room. Subject surrounded by void. Minimal, contemplative, emphasises isolation or focus.

### Layered Depth
Clear separation: foreground, midground, background. Each layer tells part of the story. Creates dimensionality and richness.

### Asymmetrical Balance
Visual weight distributed unequally but harmoniously. More sophisticated than symmetry. Feels organic, discovered rather than arranged.
