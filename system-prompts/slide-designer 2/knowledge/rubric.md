# Visual Design Rubric — Slide Evaluation

Score each slide 1-5 on all six dimensions. **Pass threshold: every dimension 4+, average 4.2+.**

Be genuinely critical. The audience is designers -- from juniors who study typography on Instagram to studio founders who've been crafting for decades. "Good enough" fails.

## Dimension 1: Typography (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Wrong font, wrong size, no hierarchy. Looks like a Google Slides default. |
| 2 | Correct fonts but poor sizing. Headlines too small or body too large. No clear focal point. |
| 3 | Decent hierarchy. Readable. But type doesn't feel considered — default tracking, generic weight choices. |
| 4 | Strong hierarchy. Intentional sizing. Good tracking and leading. Type feels designed, not placed. |
| 5 | Type IS the design. Scale, weight, position, and spacing all work together. Could be a poster. |

### What to check:
- Is there a clear primary element? (One thing should dominate)
- Is the headline large enough to read from the back of a bar?
- Is letter-spacing tightened on headlines?
- Is line-height appropriate? (Headlines tight: 0.85–0.95, body open: 1.4+)
- Are there orphaned words or awkward line breaks?
- Does the type weight create contrast? (Bold + regular, not all medium)
- Is body text constrained to a readable width? (≤900px)
- Is there extreme scale contrast? (Massive display type alongside tiny micro-text signposting)
- Does the signposting micro-text use consistent size, tracking, and positioning?

### Common failures:
- Headlines under 72px (too small for projection)
- All text the same weight
- Text spanning full 1920px width
- Line breaks that leave single words on a line
- Letter-spacing left at browser default (too loose for headlines)
- No scale contrast — everything at medium size, nothing tiny, nothing huge
- Missing or inconsistent signposting layer

## Dimension 2: Composition (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Elements randomly placed. No grid logic. Text touching edges. |
| 2 | Elements placed but with no intentional composition. Everything centered by default. |
| 3 | Reasonable layout. Safe. But no dynamism — feels template-driven. |
| 4 | Intentional composition. Good use of negative space. Elements feel anchored and purposeful. |
| 5 | Compositionally striking. Negative space is active. Asymmetry creates tension. Gallery-quality. |

### What to check:
- Is the text anchored to a clear position? (Not floating in the middle of nowhere)
- Is there intentional negative space? (Empty space that feels designed, not accidental)
- Are content margins generous? (120px+ from edges for main content)
- Is the signposting layer in the outer 40-60px zone, separate from content?
- Is the vertical position considered? (Bottom 60% reads more professional than dead-center)
- Do multiple elements relate to each other spatially?
- Is the composition asymmetric in a way that creates visual interest?
- Does this slide use a DIFFERENT composition pattern from the previous slide?

### Common failures:
- Dead-center everything (the lazy default)
- Text too close to slide edges (< 80px for content, signposting excepted)
- Equal spacing on all sides (feels mechanical, not designed)
- No clear focal point — eye doesn't know where to land
- Top-heavy layout (headline at the top, empty bottom)
- Same composition used on consecutive slides (no visual rhythm)
- Signposting missing or positioned inconsistently with other slides

## Dimension 3: Color (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Clashing colors. Poor contrast. Unreadable text. |
| 2 | Safe but generic. Pure black and white with no character. |
| 3 | Appropriate palette. Good contrast. But no accent or color moment. |
| 4 | Cohesive palette. Accent used intentionally. Colors feel curated. |
| 5 | Color is part of the storytelling. Restraint and precision. Every color choice is earned. |

### What to check:
- Is background dark enough? (#0a0a0a or similar, not medium grey)
- Is text contrast sufficient? (White on near-black, not grey on grey)
- Is accent color used for emphasis, not decoration?
- Is there only one accent per slide?
- Does the color palette feel intentional across the full deck?
- On image slides: does the overlay ensure readability?

### Common failures:
- Medium grey backgrounds (#444 or #666 — neither dark nor light, just muddy)
- Multiple competing accent colors on one slide
- Accent color on non-important elements
- No accent at all (missed opportunity for emphasis)
- Poor contrast on image backgrounds (text unreadable over busy areas)

## Dimension 4: Impact (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Forgettable. Could be any presentation. No visual presence. |
| 2 | Clean but bland. Reads as "corporate deck." |
| 3 | Solid. Looks professional. But doesn't make you pause. |
| 4 | Striking. Would hold attention in a noisy bar. Has a visual "moment." |
| 5 | Jaw-drop. The kind of slide people photograph with their phones. Unforgettable. |

### What to check:
- Would this slide hold your attention if you were standing at a bar with a drink?
- Does it have at least one element that creates visual drama?
- Is the scale of the type confident? (Big statements should be BIG)
- Does the slide have personality? (Not just "correct" — expressive)
- Would a designer in the audience think "that looks great" or "that looks fine"?

### Common failures:
- Everything at medium scale (nothing big, nothing small — just medium)
- Safe, predictable layout choices
- No tension or surprise in the composition
- Slides that all look the same (no visual rhythm across the deck)
- Placeholder energy — feels like a wireframe, not a finished slide

## Dimension 5: Craft (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Clearly auto-generated. Default spacing, default fonts, no refinement. |
| 2 | Some effort visible but rough edges. Alignment off, spacing inconsistent. |
| 3 | Clean and correct. No obvious errors. But no evidence of craft. |
| 4 | Refined. Details are polished. Spacing, alignment, and proportions feel intentional. |
| 5 | Meticulous. Every pixel considered. The kind of work that makes other designers jealous. |

### What to check:
- Are elements precisely aligned? (Not "close enough" — exactly aligned)
- Is spacing consistent where it should be and varied where it should be?
- Do text elements have refined tracking and leading?
- Are image overlays smooth and purposeful?
- Does the overall slide feel "finished" or "in progress"?
- Would you be proud to present this to a room of designers?

### Common failures:
- Alignment that's close but not exact (off by 5–10px)
- Inconsistent spacing between similar elements
- Browser-default text rendering (no tracking adjustment)
- Elements that look "placed" rather than "composed"
- Generic CSS — no evidence that a human refined it

## Dimension 6: Signposting & Micro-Detail (1–5)

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | No signposting. No micro-detail. Just content on a background. |
| 2 | Slide number exists but no consistent system. Positioned differently across slides. |
| 3 | Signposting present and consistent. But feels mechanical — just metadata, no design. |
| 4 | Signposting is a designed system. Consistent positions, refined typography, appropriate opacity. Micro-details (rules, annotations) add craft. |
| 5 | Signposting feels like an integral part of the graphic design. The tiny text, the positioning, the opacity — it's all considered. Rewards close inspection. |

### What to check:
- Is the signposting layer present on every slide?
- Are positions consistent across all slides? (Top-left, top-center, bottom-right)
- Is the signposting type small enough to be background detail? (12–14px)
- Is the opacity/color appropriate? (Visible on inspection, invisible at a glance)
- Does the section label update per act?
- Are there any micro-graphic elements (thin rules, dots, brackets) that add craft?
- On bold-color slides: does the signposting color adapt? (Dark text on light bg, light on dark)

### Common failures:
- Signposting missing on some slides but present on others
- Signposting too prominent (competes with content)
- Signposting positioned differently on different slides
- No section label — just slide number
- No micro-detail beyond the bare minimum (missed opportunity for craft)

## Evaluation Process

For each slide:

1. Take a screenshot via Chrome MCP
2. Study the screenshot as if you're a senior designer reviewing a junior's work
3. Score each dimension honestly (don't round up)
4. Calculate the average
5. If any dimension < 4 or average < 4.2: list specific issues, propose fixes, apply them
6. Re-screenshot and re-evaluate (max 3 rounds per slide)
7. If still failing after 3 rounds: flag for human review with scores and notes

### Scoring Template

```
Slide N: "[on-screen text preview]"
  Typography:   X/5 — [one-line note]
  Composition:  X/5 — [one-line note]
  Color:        X/5 — [one-line note]
  Impact:       X/5 — [one-line note]
  Craft:        X/5 — [one-line note]
  Signposting:  X/5 — [one-line note]
  Average:      X.X/5 — [PASS/FAIL]
  Issues: [list if failing]
```

### Cross-Deck Evaluation

After individual slides pass, evaluate the deck as a whole:
- **Visual rhythm:** Do slides alternate between dense and sparse? Type-heavy and image-heavy?
- **Consistency:** Does the color palette feel unified? Are fonts consistent?
- **Pacing:** Do section transitions feel distinct from content slides?
- **Crescendo:** Does the visual energy build toward Act 3 and the emotional close?
